## 0. Current Position After Paper 6

The Retained-Demand Audit Series currently consists of six completed papers.

### Paper 1 — From Usage to Retained Demand

Paper 1 established the distinction between visible usage and retained demand.

Core proposition:

> Usage ≠ Retained Demand

It decomposed retained demand into:

- inventory demand;
- collateral demand;
- liquidity-buffer demand;
- waiting liquidity.

It also separated:

- domain expansion;
- asset selection;
- asset indispensability.

### Paper 2 — Expansion Is Not Closure

Paper 2 separated infrastructure expansion from settlement closure.

Core proposition:

> Expansion ≠ Closure

It argued that stablecoins, tokenized deposits, CBDCs, internal treasury rails, omnibus structures, and orchestration mechanisms may expand the settlement field without necessarily closing all economically meaningful residual overflow.

### Paper 3 — From Retained Demand to Required Liquidity Density

Paper 3 translated retained demand into liquidity-density conditions.

Core proposition:

> Sizing ≠ Price Thesis

Sizing is treated as a conditional liquidity-feasibility test, not as price prediction.

### Paper 4 — Compression, Bypass, and Amplification

Paper 4 introduced a stack-outcome framework.

Core proposition:

> Infrastructure growth may compress, bypass, or amplify asset demand.

A stack may:

- compress asset-specific retained demand;
- bypass the asset while expanding adjacent infrastructure;
- or amplify the need to retain, mobilize, post, buffer, or maintain inventory in the asset itself.

### Paper 5 — Evidence Before Sizing

Paper 5 established an operational audit protocol.

Core proposition:

> Sizing is not rejected; sizing is gated.

The five gates are:

1. retained demand;
2. selection;
3. institutional friction;
4. liquidity-density burden;
5. removal sensitivity.

### Paper 6 — After User Abstraction

Paper 6 opened Phase II.

Paper 1 classified retained demand by type.  
Paper 6 classified retained demand by location.

Core proposition:

> Use without inventory is weak retained demand.

Paper 6 asked:

> After user-facing token exposure is abstracted away, whose balance sheet or operational system carries retained demand?

This question creates the natural basis for Paper 7 candidate work.

---

## 1. Why Paper 7 Candidate Seeds Emerged

Paper 6 introduced the distinction between:

- user-facing abstraction;
- backend retained demand;
- pre-positioned inventory;
- just-in-time liquidity.

It also clarified that user abstraction does not itself prove either demand destruction or demand relocation.

A token may disappear from the user interface because:

1. backend operators hold it;
2. operators dynamically procure it when needed;
3. stablecoins, tokenized deposits, fiat rails, or internal ledgers absorb the workflow;
4. the asset is technically supported but not retained;
5. the asset remains optional, peripheral, or replaceable.

This creates three unresolved research directions.

### 1.1 Inventory Formation

When do operators choose pre-positioned inventory instead of just-in-time procurement?

This leads to Candidate A.

### 1.2 Waiting Liquidity Compression

How far can waiting liquidity disappear through atomic settlement, netting, synchronization, liquidity-saving mechanisms, and shared ledgers?

This leads to Candidate B.

### 1.3 Algorithmic Inventory Selection

As treasury automation and AI-mediated finance expand, how might autonomous systems decide which assets to inventory, buffer, stake, post, or route through?

This leads to Candidate C.

These candidates are not forced sequels.

They are natural extensions of Paper 6’s location-based retained-demand framework.

# 2. Candidate A

## Operator-Layer Cost Compression, Inventory Formation, and Non-Selection Evidence

### Provisional Title

**Operator-Layer Cost Compression, Inventory Formation, and Non-Selection Evidence:**  
**A Boundary Model for Backend Retained Demand**

### Japanese Conceptual Title

**オペレーター層コスト圧縮・在庫形成・非選定証拠：**  
**裏側保持需要の境界モデル**

---

## 2.1 Core Question

Paper 6 established that backend retained demand becomes stronger when just-in-time liquidity is insufficient and pre-positioned inventory becomes operationally rational.

Candidate A asks:

> Under what conditions does an infrastructure-layer actor rationally choose pre-positioned inventory over just-in-time procurement?

It also asks the negative version:

> Once institutional preconditions have advanced, when does the absence of inventory formation become non-selection evidence?

This makes Candidate A the most direct continuation of Paper 6.

---

## 2.2 Boundary Model

The core boundary can be expressed as:

> JIT procurement cost  
> + execution failure risk  
> + market impact  
> + stress-time access risk  
>
> exceeds  
>
> pre-positioned inventory capital cost  
> + custody friction  
> + regulatory / accounting cost  
> + governance burden

When the first side dominates, backend retained demand becomes more plausible.

When the second side dominates, operators are more likely to avoid inventory and rely on:

- just-in-time procurement;
- substitution;
- outsourcing;
- internal netting;
- stablecoins;
- tokenized deposits;
- fiat rails;
- internal ledgers;
- prime-brokerage internalization;
- or orchestration layers.

---

## 2.3 Candidate A as a Phase II Model Paper

Candidate A would not introduce a new retained-demand category.

It would model the boundary between:

- access and possession;
- capability and retained demand;
- use and inventory;
- customer utility and asset necessity;
- acceptable delay and dangerous silence;
- unresolved evidence and non-selection evidence.

Its central contribution would be to formalize the conditions under which backend retained demand becomes rational, observable, or weakened.

---

## 2.4 Key Variables

Candidate A should examine variables such as:

- custody cost;
- key management;
- accounting treatment;
- tax treatment;
- regulatory status;
- compliance monitoring;
- internal governance burden;
- risk limits;
- balance-sheet volatility;
- hedging availability;
- market depth;
- venue fragmentation;
- market impact;
- execution failure risk;
- settlement timing risk;
- counterparty availability;
- stress-time liquidity;
- collateral lockup;
- margin eligibility;
- operational reliability requirements;
- customer experience guarantees;
- installed institutional base;
- substitution cost;
- removal sensitivity.

---

## 2.5 Non-Selection Evidence

Candidate A should include non-selection evidence as a central concept.

Non-selection evidence is not merely non-use.

It becomes meaningful when:

1. the asset is technically available;
2. institutional preconditions are sufficiently mature;
3. the relevant workflow exists;
4. alternatives are selected;
5. the asset remains unretained, optional, peripheral, or replaceable;
6. removal produces no material deterioration;
7. the pattern repeats across layers or time periods.

This allows the framework to distinguish:

- immature evidence gaps;
- acceptable delay;
- unresolved watch status;
- dangerous silence;
- and actual bypass.

## 2.7 Research Value

Candidate A has the strongest immediate research value.

It can:

- formalize Paper 6’s JIT vs inventory boundary;
- extend Paper 5’s evidence-gated logic;
- clarify when non-selection evidence becomes meaningful;
- provide a practical decision boundary for market makers, custodians, treasury operators, settlement providers, and prime-brokerage layers;
- prevent operator-layer cost compression from becoming an unfalsifiable rescue hypothesis;
- preserve the series’ core discipline: infrastructure success does not equal asset demand.

---

## 2.8 Risks

Candidate A has several risks.

- It may appear overly quantitative if the boundary model is presented with false precision.
- It may appear XRP-defensive if framed around a single asset.
- Real operator cost data may be unavailable.
- Non-selection evidence may be hard to distinguish from immature evidence.
- The model may become too abstract for general readers.
- The paper may overlap with Paper 6 if the boundary contribution is not sharpened.

To avoid these risks, Candidate A should be written as a conceptual boundary model, not as a precise numerical model.

# 3. Candidate B

## Waiting Liquidity Evaporation Conditions

### Provisional Title

**When Waiting Liquidity Disappears:**  
**Atomic Settlement, Netting, and the Compression of Temporal Liquidity Demand**

### Japanese Conceptual Title

**待機流動性はいつ消えるのか：**  
**原子決済・内部相殺・時間的流動性需要の圧縮**

---

## 3.1 Core Question

Paper 1 defined waiting liquidity as one form of retained demand.

Paper 2 identified timing and synchronization overflow as one source of residual bridge-like friction.

Paper 4 examined compression, bypass, and amplification.

Paper 6 examined just-in-time liquidity and pre-positioned inventory.

Candidate B asks:

> How far can waiting liquidity be compressed, reduced, or eliminated by advances in settlement design?

More directly:

> Under what conditions does waiting liquidity disappear, and under what conditions does it persist despite atomic settlement, netting, synchronization, or shared-ledger design?

---

## 3.2 Analytical Scope

Candidate B would examine:

- atomic settlement;
- delivery-versus-payment;
- payment-versus-payment;
- internal netting;
- liquidity-saving mechanisms;
- shared ledgers;
- synchronized settlement;
- real-time settlement;
- tokenized deposits;
- stablecoin settlement;
- CBDCs;
- omnibus structures;
- prime-brokerage internalization;
- orchestration layers;
- settlement timing gaps;
- legal finality;
- operating hours;
- cross-ledger coordination;
- jurisdictional mismatch;
- participant asynchrony;
- compliance or collateral checks;
- stress-time fragmentation.

---

## 3.3 Core Hypothesis

Waiting liquidity can be compressed by technology and settlement design.

However, it may not fully disappear.

It may persist where there are:

- different jurisdictions;
- different ledgers;
- different operating hours;
- legal-finality differences;
- compliance checks;
- AML / CFT controls;
- collateral eligibility checks;
- credit risk;
- participant asynchrony;
- settlement windows;
- stress-time liquidity fragmentation;
- operational failure risk;
- non-synchronized institutional processes.

The key question is not whether settlement can become faster.

The key question is whether temporal capital immobilization disappears under real institutional conditions.

---

## 3.4 Research Value

Candidate B has high conceptual and policy relevance.

It can:

- deepen Paper 1’s waiting-liquidity category;
- extend Paper 2’s timing and synchronization overflow;
- connect Paper 4’s compression logic to temporal liquidity demand;
- connect Paper 6’s JIT/inventory boundary to settlement timing;
- speak to FMI, settlement-design, stablecoin, tokenized-deposit, CBDC, and payment-system audiences;
- reduce XRP-specific overreading by focusing on general settlement architecture.

Candidate B may be the most elegant infrastructure-design paper among the candidates.

---

## 3.5 Risks

Candidate B is broader and more demanding than Candidate A.

Risks include:

- scope expansion into payment-system literature;
- need for stronger BIS / CPMI / PFMI / settlement-finality references;
- overlap with Paper 2 and Paper 4;
- excessive abstraction;
- difficulty keeping the paper connected to retained-demand logic;
- risk of becoming a general settlement paper rather than a series paper.

---

## 3.6 Launch Conditions

Candidate B should remain on hold until:

1. Paper 1–6 routing is stable.
2. Paper 4 overlap is clearly managed.
3. Waiting liquidity is redefined cleanly for Phase II.
4. Settlement-design references are organized.
5. The paper can be framed as retained-demand analysis rather than general FMI commentary.
6. The argument can remain neutral toward specific assets.
7. The compression-vs-persistence structure is sharply defined.

---

## 3.7 Current Status

**Priority:** 2  
**Status:** Secondary Hold Candidate  
**Current Judgment:** Strong and elegant, but broader than Candidate A and not ready for immediate launch.

# 4. Candidate C

## Smart Retention / Autonomous Treasury

### Provisional Title

**Smart Retention:**  
**Autonomous Treasury, Agentic Finance, and Algorithmic Inventory Selection in Digital Asset Infrastructure**

### Japanese Conceptual Title

**スマート保持需要：**  
**自律型財務・AIエージェント金融・デジタル資産インフラにおける在庫選定アルゴリズム**

---

## 4.1 Core Question

Paper 6 asked:

> Who carries retained demand after user-facing token exposure disappears?

Candidate C pushes this question further:

> What happens when the decision to hold, route, buffer, stake, post, or inventory an asset is made by automated treasury systems or AI-mediated financial agents?

In other words:

> Which assets do autonomous systems decide to hold, how much do they hold, and why?

---

## 4.2 Analytical Scope

Candidate C would examine:

- autonomous treasury;
- AI agents;
- agentic finance;
- programmable finance;
- treasury automation;
- liquidity routing;
- real-time rebalancing;
- risk-adjusted inventory selection;
- collateral optimization;
- agent commerce;
- wallet abstraction;
- paymaster automation;
- algorithmic liquidity buffers;
- automated settlement routing;
- machine-executed treasury policies;
- execution reliability scoring;
- smart collateral selection;
- dynamic inventory targets.

---

## 4.3 Core Hypothesis

Future retained demand may increasingly arise from operational algorithms rather than explicit human holding decisions.

Autonomous treasury systems may compare assets according to:

- execution failure risk;
- slippage;
- market depth;
- custody cost;
- regulatory treatment;
- accounting burden;
- collateral eligibility;
- stress-time access;
- substitutability;
- expected waiting time;
- hedgeability;
- internal policy constraints;
- counterparty availability;
- jurisdictional compatibility;
- operational reliability;
- opportunity cost.

In this environment, retained demand becomes less about what users want to hold and more about what systems rationally maintain for execution, resilience, collateral, and routing.

---

## 4.4 Research Value

Candidate C has the greatest long-term upside.

It can:

- extend Paper 6 from backend retained demand to autonomous retained demand;
- connect retained-demand analysis to AI agents and autonomous finance;
- link digital-asset infrastructure to machine-executed treasury logic;
- create a bridge between the Retained-Demand Audit Series and broader human-AI / AI-operational-system research;
- provide a future framework for agent commerce and programmable liquidity.

It may eventually become the most original Phase II extension.

---

## 4.5 Risks

Candidate C is the least ready to launch.

Risks include:

- evidence is currently thin;
- the topic may become speculative;
- it may sound like generic “AI will manage finance” futurism;
- boundaries with AAS / ALISE / Human-AI Research OS work may blur;
- it may drift away from retained-demand audit discipline;
- it may require examples that do not yet exist publicly.

Candidate C should not be started until there is stronger real-world evidence of autonomous treasury, AI-driven liquidity routing, or machine-executed inventory selection.

---

## 4.6 Launch Conditions

Candidate C should remain on long hold until:

1. autonomous treasury examples become more visible;
2. AI-mediated liquidity routing becomes operational rather than conceptual;
3. paymaster automation, wallet abstraction, or agent commerce creates observable backend inventory behavior;
4. treasury automation begins to show asset-selection logic;
5. the paper can be written as retained-demand audit rather than AI futurism;
6. it can be clearly separated from AAS / ALISE methodology work;
7. the evidence base is sufficient to avoid speculative overreach.

---

## 4.7 Current Status

**Priority:** 3  
**Status:** Long Hold Candidate  
**Current Judgment:** Highest future originality, but not suitable for near-term launch.
