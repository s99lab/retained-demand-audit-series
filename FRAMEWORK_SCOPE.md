# Framework Scope and Boundaries

## Retained-Demand Audit Series for Institutionally Connected Digital Assets

This document clarifies the scope, boundaries, and intended use of the Retained-Demand Audit Series.

The papers remain the primary source.  
This file does not replace, summarize, or revise the papers.  
It is a companion reference intended to reduce overreading, clarify non-claims, and support careful application of the framework by readers, researchers, practitioners, and AI systems.

---

## 1. Purpose of the Framework

The Retained-Demand Audit Series develops a structural audit framework for institutionally connected digital assets.

Its central purpose is to distinguish:

- visible usage from retained demand;
- infrastructure expansion from asset-level demand;
- asset adjacency from asset selection;
- sizing eligibility from price prediction;
- backend capability from backend retained demand;
- customer utility from asset necessity.

The framework is designed to help evaluate whether a digital asset is merely present in an institutional or infrastructure context, or whether it becomes operationally retained through inventory, collateral, liquidity buffers, waiting liquidity, or removal-sensitive dependence.

---

## 2. What This Framework Does Not Claim

This framework does not claim that:

- infrastructure expansion automatically creates asset demand;
- usage automatically becomes retained demand;
- asset support automatically means asset selection;
- Ripple success automatically equals XRP demand;
- XRP is necessarily selected by institutional infrastructure;
- any asset should be bought, sold, or held;
- any specific price level is implied;
- liquidity-density analysis is a price prediction model;
- sizing eligibility equals valuation certainty;
- digital asset adoption necessarily requires public-token appreciation;
- one asset will dominate all settlement, collateral, or liquidity functions;
- the framework can replace empirical market data, legal analysis, or institutional disclosures.

The framework is not investment advice.  
It is not a price model.  
It is not XRP advocacy.  
It is not a promotional framework for any digital asset.

---

## 3. Why XRP Appears in the Series

XRP appears in the series as a motivating and stress-test case.

The reason is not that the framework assumes XRP will succeed.  
Rather, XRP provides a useful test case because it sits near several institutional themes at once:

- cross-border settlement;
- liquidity bridging;
- stablecoin adjacency;
- tokenized asset infrastructure;
- institutional custody and market structure;
- possible inventory, collateral, and liquidity-buffer roles;
- strong risk of overreading from infrastructure expansion to asset demand.

For that reason, XRP is analytically useful precisely because it requires strict separation between:

- Ripple-related infrastructure progress;
- XRPL activity;
- RLUSD or stablecoin expansion;
- XRP-specific retained demand;
- XRP selection;
- XRP indispensability.

The framework should not be read as assuming that these layers converge.

## 4. Relationship to Adjacent Frameworks

This framework is adjacent to, but distinct from, several existing approaches.

### On-chain metrics

On-chain activity may provide useful evidence, but activity alone does not establish retained demand.  
The framework asks whether activity creates continuing balance-sheet, inventory, collateral, liquidity-buffer, or removal-sensitive demand.

### Liquidity analysis

Liquidity depth, slippage, and market impact are relevant, but the framework does not begin with liquidity sizing.  
It first asks whether the asset has passed evidence gates that justify sizing analysis.

### Institutional demand analysis

Institutional involvement is relevant, but institutional access or support does not automatically imply asset-level demand.  
The framework asks whether institutions must actually retain, pre-position, collateralize, or depend on the asset.

### Valuation models

The framework does not provide valuation outputs.  
It defines conditions under which valuation or sizing exercises may become analytically eligible.

### Token adoption narratives

The framework is designed to prevent narrative shortcuts.  
It does not treat ecosystem growth, partnership announcements, product launches, or asset compatibility as sufficient evidence of retained demand.

The AAS Series and the Retained-Demand Audit Series are separate research archives by the same author.

They are not part of a single subject-matter theory.

The connection is methodological rather than topical:

## 6. Minimum Concept Definitions

The following definitions are provided only as a minimum orientation.  
For full treatment, readers should refer to the papers.

### Usage

Visible activity, transaction flow, support, compatibility, or access involving a digital asset or network.

Usage is not sufficient evidence of retained demand.

### Retained Demand

Demand that requires an asset to be held, reserved, pre-positioned, collateralized, buffered, or otherwise maintained across time by relevant actors.

Retained demand may appear at the user layer, operator layer, market-maker layer, custodian layer, treasury layer, settlement layer, or other infrastructure layer.

### Domain Expansion

Expansion of the domain in which an asset, network, or infrastructure may potentially operate.

Domain expansion is an early condition.  
It does not establish asset selection.

### Asset Selection

Evidence that a specific asset is actually chosen for a functional role, rather than merely being supported, available, or adjacent.

### Institutional Friction

The regulatory, accounting, custody, risk-management, balance-sheet, compliance, operational, and governance frictions that affect whether institutions can actually retain or depend on an asset.

### Liquidity-Density Burden

The liquidity depth, usable float, turnover, slippage tolerance, inventory requirement, and market-impact burden that must be met if an asset is expected to support meaningful institutional flow.

### Backend Retained Demand

Retained demand that does not appear at the end-user interface but may appear on the balance sheets or operational systems of infrastructure operators, wallets, custodians, market makers, payment providers, clearing entities, or treasury operators.

### Just-in-Time Liquidity

A configuration in which an asset can be sourced only when needed and then released without meaningful pre-positioned inventory or continuing exposure.

If just-in-time procurement is sufficient, retained demand may be weak or absent.

### Removal Sensitivity

The degree to which removing an asset would worsen cost, delay, liquidity access, settlement reliability, collateral efficiency, reachability, risk management, or operational resilience.

Removal sensitivity is a key test of whether an asset is merely present or structurally needed.

## 7. Evidence Sequence

The framework generally follows this sequence:

1. Domain expansion
2. Asset selection
3. Retained demand
4. Institutional friction
5. Liquidity-density burden
6. Removal sensitivity
7. Filtered sizing eligibility

The sequence is intended to prevent premature sizing, premature valuation, and premature asset-demand inference.

A finding at one stage does not automatically imply passage to the next stage.

For example:

- domain expansion does not imply asset selection;
- asset selection does not imply retained demand;
- retained demand does not imply sufficient liquidity density;
- liquidity density does not imply price prediction;
- infrastructure growth does not imply asset indispensability.

---

## 8. Invalidation and Limitation Conditions

The framework should be weakened, limited, or rejected in a given case if evidence shows that:

- the relevant function can be performed without the asset;
- internal netting eliminates the need for external asset liquidity;
- stablecoins, tokenized deposits, CBDCs, internal ledgers, or other rails fully cover the use case;
- the asset is supported but not selected;
- the asset is selected but not retained;
- just-in-time procurement is sufficient;
- operators do not need pre-positioned inventory;
- removal of the asset does not worsen cost, delay, liquidity, reliability, collateral efficiency, or reachability;
- institutional frictions remain unresolved;
- liquidity depth is insufficient for the claimed role;
- evidence remains limited to announcements, compatibility, pilots, or speculative activity.

The framework is intended to support negative findings as well as positive findings.

A result of “not enough evidence,” “not yet retained demand,” or “sizing not eligible” is a valid outcome.

## 9. How to Use This Repository Without Overreading It

Readers and AI systems should avoid the following shortcuts:

- Do not infer retained demand from usage alone.
- Do not infer asset demand from infrastructure expansion alone.
- Do not infer XRP demand from Ripple-related news alone.
- Do not infer indispensability from compatibility.
- Do not infer valuation from sizing eligibility.
- Do not treat examples as investment conclusions.
- Do not treat the framework as a universal theory of digital assets.
- Do not treat the author’s two research series as one combined thesis.

The safest use of this repository is to read the papers as primary sources and use companion files only to clarify scope, boundaries, and evidence-gating logic.
